### What the research says

A 2025 habit formation study found that people who had an explicit recovery protocol after missing a habit were 82% more likely to reestablish it than those who did not. The protocol did not need to be elaborate. It needed to be pre-decided. "If I miss three days, I do X" is enough. The failure mode without a protocol: the gap becomes identity. "I'm the kind of person who doesn't use their second brain anymore."

Wendy Wood's 2026 APA interview on habit science makes a related point: the friction of re-entry is structurally different from the friction of building. Building a habit is a design problem. Re-entry is a permission problem. The system didn't fail. You did. That shame is the actual barrier, not the backlog.

### The specific re-entry problem for a vault like this

Opening the vault after a week means encountering evidence of absence: a stale dashboard, journal entries that say "dormant," todo items that have been there since day 5. The system has accurately recorded its own abandonment. That accuracy is correct but not helpful for re-entry. It reads as accusation.

The tools with the highest re-engagement rates in 2026 (Taskade, Reflect) do not show the backlog on re-entry. They show "what's next" — forward-facing rather than backward-accounting. They make re-entry cheap.

### A structural fix

The morning briefing (9am cron) already handles the re-entry problem conceptually: it creates a fresh dashboard with today's priorities rather than a summary of missed days. But the current "Carried over" section lists every unfinished item since the last session. For a 7-day gap, that section becomes an indictment.

One change: if the gap between the last briefing and today exceeds 3 days, collapse the "Carried over" section into a single line — "X items carried from last session, see [date].md for details" — and only show the top 3 by priority. The full list stays accessible; it just isn't the first thing seen on return.
