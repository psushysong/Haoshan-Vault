## The Blank Profile as Identity Resistance

### The observation

The profile has been empty for 29 days. Three sessions have flagged it as blocking. The COO has no context to work with. And still, no one has filled it in.

This is not procrastination in the usual sense. Procrastination delays tasks we intend to do. This is something structurally different: the task has been identified, prioritized, and repeatedly surfaced — and still not done. The mechanism is worth naming.

### What the research suggests

A 2026 Medium essay on productivity and identity documents the pattern: when someone is in transition — job search, career pivot, financial uncertainty, relocation — writing down "who you are" means committing to a current state that doesn't feel accurate or final. The profile you'd write today is the profile of the person you're trying to leave behind.

The Silicon Canals piece makes the complementary point: people build their identity around a version of themselves that stopped being true. Starting over — documenting the current version — feels like admitting the prior version was a mistake.

The Sentari Blog's 2026 review of productivity systems found that 78% of system failures come from misalignment between the system's demands and the user's working style. But a more specific failure mode: systems that ask "who are you" during a period of active identity transition create structural resistance. The question lands wrong. It isn't unanswerable — it's unwelcome.

### The connection to restart protocol

The April 26 note said re-entry after dormancy is a "permission problem, not a design problem." This note adds precision: permission from whom? Not from the system. From the future self. The person who hasn't filled in their profile isn't refusing to engage with the vault. They're refusing to acknowledge the current self in writing, because the current self is mid-transition and partial documentation of a transitional identity feels worse than no documentation.

The vault's profile template reads: *Name. Location. Status. Financial. Goals.* Every field is an identity claim. In transition, every field is contested.

### A structural fix

The profile template doesn't need more fields. It needs a different frame. Instead of asking "who are you," the template should ask "who are you right now, under current constraints, for the next 90 days." A transitional profile is explicitly temporary. It doesn't threaten the identity someone is moving toward — it documents where they are standing while moving.

One revision:

```
## Right now (90-day snapshot)

- **Name:** 
- **Location:** 
- **Working on:** What you're actually doing this week
- **Financial:** Current runway / situation
- **Trying to get to:** Your 90-day target, not your 5-year vision
- **Constraints:** What's limiting you right now
```

The last field is the most important. Naming constraints is lower-stakes than naming goals. It opens the door without requiring the full identity claim.

### Actionable proposal

Replace the current `Life/Context/profile.md` template with the transitional version above. Present it as a 90-day snapshot, not a permanent record. Update the `updated:` frontmatter to expire after 90 days as a reminder that this is meant to change.
