### What he built

Andrej Karpathy described a self-maintaining wiki where the LLM does the writing, linking, categorizing, and consistency-checking. One research topic grew to ~100 articles and 400,000 words without him writing a word directly. The raw material goes in; the LLM turns it into a structured, cross-linked reference.

### Why it's relevant here

This vault runs a COO that reads files and executes tasks. Karpathy's setup runs an LLM that reads raw notes and writes the wiki. Same underlying move: the human generates messy material, the LLM imposes structure.

The difference: this vault uses the LLM as an *agent* (it takes actions, tracks tasks, makes decisions). Karpathy's setup uses it as a *writer* (it transforms input into structured output, no task-taking). Both approaches assume the LLM is better at maintaining consistency than the human is.

### Connection to this vault

The HARVEST step in `cron-prompts/morning-discovery.md` is the embryo of this. Right now it only flags notes that are ready to become output. Karpathy's approach would have the LLM *write* the output directly from raw Notes/.

Worth thinking about: should Notes/ stay human-only (as the vault currently rules), or is there a second folder where the LLM can draft from raw Notes/ material? A `Drafts/` folder that the LLM writes and the human edits.

### The broader 2026 pattern

Multiple sources confirm the shift: AI assistants moving from reactive (answer when asked) to proactive (anticipate, summarize, execute). The vault's cron system is ahead of most personal setups on this axis. The gap is in memory — the vault doesn't yet have semantic search or embeddings, so the COO can only work with what it explicitly reads.

### Possible next move

If the vault grows, the COO will spend too much time reading files to find what's relevant. A lightweight semantic index (even a plain `index.md` that the morning discovery cron maintains) would let the COO skip to relevant material faster.
